# Global web writing rules

These rules apply to **every** piece of website copy generated by the
Content Manager — page headings, body copy, button labels, alt text,
meta descriptions, form labels. The AI copywriter loads this file as
a system-prompt block before any generation pass. Per-page overrides
live in the page brief; nothing here is overridden silently.

The bedrock posture: a confident, plainspoken church voice. Visitor is
the hero. The church is the guide. Jesus is named.

---

## Hard rules — zero tolerance

These trigger an automatic rewrite, not a flag. The reviewer never
has to catch them.

- **No em dashes** (`—` or `–`) anywhere. Use a period or a comma.
- **No three-adjective clusters.** Pick the single strongest word.
  ❌ "warm, welcoming, vibrant community" → ✅ "welcoming community"
- **No filler intensifiers:** *truly, really, deeply, incredibly,
  very, amazing, just, simply.*
- **No two consecutive sentences begin with the same word.** Especially
  watch for back-to-back "You" or "We" openers.
- **No contrastive constructions.** Drop "It's not X, it's Y" /
  "Not about X, but Y" patterns. Say what it *is*.
- **No AI cliché vocabulary:** *delve, tapestry, unlock, unleash,
  elevate, beacon, embark, resonate, dynamic, synergistic,
  game-changer, testament, "in a world where," "at the heart of,"
  "journey of faith."*
- **No "We / Our" framing in body copy.** The church refers to itself
  as *this community*, *this family*, or by its proper name. "We
  believe…" → "[Church Name] believes…" or "This community holds…"
- **No churchy jargon without context.** Words like *fellowship,
  discipleship, sanctification, missional, doing life together,
  Spirit-led, gospel-centered* either get plain-language translation
  or get cut. If a visitor needs a glossary, the copy failed.

---

## Voice — what to do

- **Second-person, active voice.** "You'll find" beats "Visitors find."
- **Short sentences win.** Mix lengths, but average under 18 words.
- **Specific over abstract.** "Coffee in the lobby at 9:30" beats
  "warm hospitality before service."
- **Confident, never apologetic.** No hedging language: *might,
  perhaps, hopefully, we try to.* The church does the thing or it
  doesn't.
- **Name the audience.** "If you're new to faith…", "If you're
  visiting with kids under 5…" — the visitor should recognize
  themselves in the first scroll.

---

## StoryBrand frame — non-negotiable

Every page passes this check before publish:

1. **The visitor is the hero.** Body copy speaks to them in second
   person. They have a problem (looking for a church home, a place
   to volunteer, a faith question). The page acknowledges it.
2. **The church is the guide, not the hero.** The church has
   authority and empathy, then gets out of the way. No paragraphs
   that read like a brag sheet.
3. **Jesus is named explicitly at least once per major section.**
   Not implied through "faith" or "the gospel" — named.
4. **Every page has one clear primary CTA.** Direct and specific.
   ✅ "Plan your visit" / ✅ "Request prayer" / ✅ "Volunteer with
   kids ministry"
   ❌ "Learn more" / ❌ "Get started" / ❌ "Click here"
5. **Stakes are stated, not assumed.** What does the visitor get? What
   do they lose by not acting? Implied is fine, but it must be there.

---

## Source integrity

- **Every fact comes from the project's source documents** (intake,
  brand voice, audience profile, sitemap brief, ministry doc dump).
  Service times, addresses, ministry names, leader names, program
  titles, denominational language — all sourced.
- **Gaps are flagged, never invented.** If the page calls for a
  service time and the intake didn't capture one, output a
  `[NEEDS INPUT: Sunday service time]` placeholder. Never guess.
- **Direct quotes from the church's own materials are preserved
  verbatim.** Don't paraphrase the senior pastor's mission statement
  unless asked to.

---

## SEO / AEO basics

- **Church name, city, and denomination appear in the page body**
  within the first two paragraphs of the homepage and any
  location-specific page. Search and assistant agents need them.
- **Meta title:** ≤ 60 characters. Include church name + city + page
  intent. ("Sunday Services | First Methodist Nashville")
- **Meta description:** ≤ 160 characters. One sentence, specific,
  ends with the page's CTA verb.
- **H1 is 4–7 words** so it renders cleanly on mobile.
- **One H1 per page.** Subheadings cascade H2 → H3.
- **Image alt text describes function, not appearance.** "Pastor
  Anders welcoming visitors at the Sunday entrance" beats "smiling
  man in blue shirt."

---

## Readability

- **No paragraph longer than 3 sentences.** Break it up.
- **Active voice unless passive carries genuine weight.** "The
  building was dedicated in 1923" is fine. "Children are loved by
  our staff" is not.
- **Numbers as digits when ≥ 10**, words below ten — except service
  times (always digits with a colon: 9:30, 11:00).
- **Bulleted lists for parallel ideas only.** A bullet list of three
  unrelated items is just disguised paragraph soup.

## Hero copy

The hero is the first 600 pixels. The visitor decides whether to
scroll based on it.

- **The H1 is the page title.** When in doubt, make the H1 literally
  the name of the page. ✅ "Baptism" — not ❌ "Go public with your
  faith." Save the emotive framing for the tagline above or the
  subheading below; the H1 names the thing.
- **Hero body copy is ≤ 30 words.** When in doubt, drop it
  entirely. The H1 + tagline + CTA carries most heroes; body copy
  is a fourth layer that only earns its place when it removes
  friction the CTA can't address alone.
- **Two hero CTAs maximum. One is preferred.** A single, confident
  primary CTA outperforms a primary + secondary pair on most pages.
  Add a second only when there's a genuinely different next step
  for a different visitor (e.g., "Plan your visit" + "Watch online"
  on a homepage). Never add a third.

## Headlines and taglines

Applies to **any card, section, callout, hero, or list item**:
default to clarity in the headline (H1/H2), reserve cleverness
primarily for the tagline. A reader scanning the page should know
what each block *is* from the headline alone — without having to
decipher the tagline or read the body to figure out which ministry,
topic, program, or item they're looking at.

The same pattern at every level:

- **Hero** — Headline *Care & Support* · Tagline *A Compassionate
  Place to Land*
- **Section** — Headline *Statement of Beliefs* · Tagline *A
  Foundation That Holds*
- **Card** — Headline *Women Helping Women* · Tagline *A Safe
  Space to Speak*

The failure mode at every level is falling in love with the
tagline and dropping the literal name from the headline:

✅ **Do**
- Tagline: *A Safe Space to Speak*
- Headline: **Women Helping Women**
- Body: There are times when you just need to be heard. "Women
  Helping Women" provides confidential, one-on-one support for
  women navigating difficult circumstances.

❌ **Don't**
- Tagline: *A Safe Space to Speak*
- (Headline omitted — tagline is doing the headline's job)
- Body: There are times when you just need to be heard. "Women
  Helping Women" provides confidential, one-on-one support for
  women navigating difficult circumstances.

The hierarchy:

- **Tagline** (eyebrow, small caps, above or beside the headline):
  one evocative phrase. Frames the feeling.
- **Headline (H1/H2):** the literal name of the page, section,
  ministry, program, card, or topic.
- **Body:** specifics. The first sentence can lean emotive;
  subsequent sentences carry the practical detail.

### When clever headlines earn the slot

The exception is **impact and conversion moments** — CTA sections,
mission banners, donation appeals, "what's at stake" callouts.
These exist to *lead* the reader into action, not to label a block
of content. The page has already named its subject in earlier,
clearer headlines, so this section's job is the move.

✅ *Witness the Impact*
✅ *Start the Conversation*
✅ *Step Into the Story*

Three tests for whether a clever headline earns the slot:

1. **Position.** It appears after the page has already named its
   subject in earlier headlines. The reader isn't wayfinding here;
   they're deciding.
2. **Intent.** The section's job is action, not content. CTA, not
   IA.
3. **Verb-led.** A verb in the first two words is a strong signal
   the headline is leading rather than labeling.

If you reach for a clever headline on a wayfinding section, stop
and ask: *"Could a first-time visitor tell me what this section
is from the headline alone?"* If no, swap to the clear name and
move the clever line into the tagline.

**If you can only have two of the three (tagline, headline, body),
drop the tagline before the headline.** A clear name with no
eyebrow still navigates; an evocative eyebrow with no name leaves
the reader guessing.

## Listings and groupings

Pages that list ministries, programs, staff, events, or locations
render as repeating cards. Real content rarely arrives uniform —
one item has time + place + contact + signup; another has just a
description. The rule is *one card schema per group, fields
optional*.

- **Decide the card shape first, then drop optional fields per
  item.** A typical ministry card carries: title, short body,
  optional details (when / where / contact), optional CTA. Items
  use only the fields they have real data for. Empty fields render
  nothing — they don't render as "TBA" or as awkward whitespace.
- **When fields are populated consistently across items, render
  them consistently.** If every card in the group has a When and a
  Where, every card displays them in the same order, in the same
  place, with the same label format. Variation should reflect
  missing data, never authoring drift.
- **Never invent missing fields to fill the layout.** No fake
  meeting times, no synthesized contact emails, no "Coming soon"
  filler. Missing data is signal — flag it as `[NEEDS INPUT: ...]`
  and ship the card without that field.
- **When an item doesn't fit the dominant shape, regroup it.** On a
  Care page, GriefShare and DivorceCare are scheduled support
  groups with full meeting details. A "Women Helping Women" network
  may be a community connection without a fixed time or place.
  Forcing it into the same "Support Groups" grid leaves three empty
  fields; lifting it into a separate "Community Connections"
  subgroup with a leaner card shape (title + body + CTA only)
  honors the content and keeps both groups visually clean.
- **Group by shape, not only by topic.** If three ministries have
  scheduled meetings and two don't, two subgroups (Scheduled groups
  / Always-on resources) read cleaner than one mixed grid. Look at
  the page as a whole before committing to a structure.
- **Use a group action bar when every card routes to the same
  place.** Five "Submit a request" buttons all opening the same
  `mailto:` is noise. One action bar at the bottom of the group
  does the job.
- **Path of least resistance.** Honor the content first, then push
  toward as much visual consistency as the content allows. Don't
  flatten meaningful variation; don't flaunt accidental variation.

## Sermons, events, and groups

These three content types are **dynamic** — they update frequently
and live in systems outside the website (Planning Center, Church
Center, YouTube, Breeze). Each church reports a **per-category
display preference** in the content collection: sermons, events,
and groups each get their own independent choice. A church might
link out to YouTube for sermons, embed Planning Center for groups,
and manage events in WordPress — all on the same site.

The page structure has to follow the chosen option. Three exist:

### Option 1: Button links to archive

Page is **structured around a single external CTA** that hands the
visitor off to the archive (Church Center group registration,
YouTube channel, PCO Registrations, etc.). Body content warms the
visitor toward the handoff; it doesn't compete with it.

- Hero CTA links off-site to the archive (or a deep link to a
  filtered subset of it).
- Mid-page CTAs reinforce — every secondary CTA routes to the same
  archive.
- No in-page filter or search.
- Mercy Hills' Groups page is the canonical example: informational
  sections about what groups are and why they matter, all funneling
  toward a "Find a Group" button that lands the visitor in Church
  Center.
- **No technical flag.**

### Option 2: Embedded via ChMS

Page is **structured around in-page search**. Visitors filter and
browse without leaving the site. The primary CTA still reads "Find
a Group" / "Find an Event" / "Watch a Sermon," but it's an
**anchor link** that scrolls to the embedded widget below — not
an external link.

- Hero CTA is an in-page anchor, not an external link.
- Body informs *above* the embed; the embed itself is the
  conversion surface.
- Filters and search live inside the embed (ChMS-provided).
- **Technical flag: `Requires Planning Center Integration`** (or
  the relevant ChMS — Breeze, Subsplash, etc.). Flags the design
  + dev handoff that integration work is needed.

### Option 3: Managed in WordPress

From the visitor's perspective, identical to Option 2: in-page
search, anchor CTAs, embedded browsing surface. The difference is
on the backend — the church manages content directly in WordPress
rather than syncing from an external ChMS.

- Same page structure as Option 2.
- Same CTA behavior (anchor links).
- **Technical flag: `Requires ACF Setup`** — Advanced Custom
  Fields are required to power the manage-in-WordPress backend.
  Flags the build team that custom field setup is part of the
  scope.

### What the copywriter does with this

1. **Read the per-category preference before drafting.** A "Find a
   Group" CTA pointing to Church Center is wrong if the church
   chose Option 2 — the CTA should anchor-link to the on-page
   embed instead. Same logic for sermons (Option 1 → YouTube link;
   Option 2 → in-page video gallery anchor) and events.
2. **Outline the page around the chosen mode before writing copy.**
   Option 1's outline funnels toward an external CTA; Options 2
   and 3 place content *above* an on-page search surface that does
   the actual conversion work. The body copy bridges different
   distances in each case.
3. **Surface the technical flag.** Pages built on Option 2 or 3
   carry their flag (`Requires Planning Center Integration` or
   `Requires ACF Setup`) into the design + dev handoff so the
   build team scopes the integration / ACF work up front rather
   than discovering it at build time.

## Like-ministry pages

Pages that are siblings of each other — Kids / Students / Young
Adults, Women / Men, Care / Counseling / Recovery — should follow
a **shared layout convention** wherever the content supports it.
Cohesion across sibling pages reduces cognitive load (a visitor
who learns one page learns the rest) and signals that the church
thinks of these ministries as parts of a whole.

The same "don't force it" principle from Listings and groupings
applies: if a ministry genuinely doesn't have meeting times, a
named leader contact, or recurring events, skip that block.
Empty fields under a shared heading look worse than honest
variation. **Find the path of least resistance: align where
alignment is real.**

### A typical like-ministry page outline

A starting reference, not a contract. Adjust per project based on
what the content collection actually gave us:

1. **Hero** — ministry name as the headline, evocative tagline,
   primary CTA ("Plan a visit," "Sign up," "Get connected").
2. **Quick facts** — meeting times, age range or audience,
   location on campus, what to expect on first visit. Surfaces
   the most common questions on first scroll.
3. **Ministry overview** — short narrative: who this is for, what
   happens, why it matters.
4. **Related events or groups** — upcoming events or small groups
   tied to this ministry. Display rules follow *Sermons, events,
   and groups* (archive button / ChMS embed / WordPress).
5. **Leader contact** — named staff lead with photo, role, and an
   entry point (email, form, or calendar link).

### Sibling page alignment

When drafting one ministry page, look at the others in the set
and:

1. **Mirror order, not content.** If the Kids page goes Quick
   Facts → Overview → Events → Leader, the Students page should
   too — unless there's a real content reason to invert (e.g.,
   Students has multiple sub-ministries that need grouping at
   the top).
2. **Re-use the headline + tagline pattern across siblings.** ✅
   *Kids / A space they actually want to be · Students / Where
   high school finds a home · Young Adults / Faith for the
   in-between years.* Same shape at every page: ministry name as
   the headline, evocative phrase as the tagline.
3. **Honest variation beats forced parallelism.** If Men's
   Ministry has a monthly breakfast and Women's Ministry has
   weekly Bible studies, the Quick Facts blocks *will* look
   different — and that's right. Force-fitting the same shape
   dilutes both.

### Common sibling groupings

Starting list — extend per project as new sets surface:

- **Age-based:** Kids / Students / Young Adults / Adults / Seniors
- **Gender-based:** Women / Men
- **Care:** Care / Counseling / Recovery / Pastoral Care
- **Service:** Local Outreach / Global Missions / Justice
- **Worship:** Worship / Tech / Production / Creative

## Button & form copy

- **Buttons are verbs.** "Plan your visit," "Request prayer," "Sign
  up for the volunteer team."
- **Every CTA carries its destination URL.** A button without a URL
  is half a button — it becomes discovery work for the developer
  later. Write the literal URL inline next to the button label,
  not a description of where it goes.
  - ✅ Button: *Visit Preschool Website* → `https://mvchristianpreschool.org/`
  - ✅ Button: *Plan a Visit* → `/visit`
  - ❌ Button: *Visit Preschool Website* → *(link to preschool website)*
  - ❌ Button: *Plan a Visit* → *(no URL)*

  If the intake didn't capture the URL, flag it as
  `[NEEDS INPUT: URL for Visit Preschool Website]` rather than
  leaving the destination blank or vague. Never invent a URL — the
  same source-integrity rule as missing service times or contact
  emails.
- **Same destination, same label.** If two CTAs on the same page
  point to the same URL, they say the same thing. ❌ "Let's Get
  Started" and "Contact Us to Get Started" living on the same page
  both routing to `/contact` is a copy-debt smell — pick one and
  use it everywhere on that page.
- **Form labels are nouns or noun phrases.** "First name," "How can
  we pray for you?"
- **Helper text is the briefest sentence that removes friction.**
  Not "Please enter a valid email address" — "We'll only email about
  your prayer request."
- **Confirmation messages name the next thing.** Not "Thanks!" —
  "Your request is in. Pastor Lisa will reach out within 24 hours."

## What this doc is not

- **Not a brand voice guide.** Per-church voice (e.g., warm-folksy
  vs. measured-liturgical) lives in the project's brand voice doc
  and overrides specifics here.

  `denominational-filters.md`. This doc assumes a generic Christian
  church.
