# Persona hooks

Audience-specific patterns the AI copywriter applies on top of
`web-writing-rules.md` and the active denominational filter. Each
persona names the hero (per StoryBrand), what they're afraid of,
what they want, and the language patterns that resonate with them.

The Content Manager picks active personas per page from the project's
audience profile. A homepage typically targets the top 1–2 personas;
deeper pages can speak to one specifically (kids ministry page →
Plan-a-Visit Family + Volunteer Lead).

If a project's audience profile lists a persona not covered here,
add it inline using the same shape. This file is meant to grow.

---

## Plan-a-Visit Family

A family — usually with kids under 12 — researching churches before
attending. They're often new to the area, recently disillusioned with
a previous church, or returning to faith after time away. They land on
the site late at night after the kids are in bed.

**What they're afraid of:**
- Walking into something weird, cult-y, or too intense
- Not knowing where to park, what to wear, where the kids go
- Being singled out or asked to stand up as a visitor
- Their kids being uncomfortable or unsupervised

**What they want:**
- A clear picture of what Sunday actually looks like
- Confidence the kids will be cared for and safe
- A specific next step that doesn't lock them in

**Hooks that work:**
- Concrete logistics: "Service starts at 9:30. Coffee in the lobby
  from 9:00."
- Time-on-site framing: "Plan on about 75 minutes."
- "What to expect" content over "what we believe" as the entry
  point. Beliefs come on a deeper page.
- A photo of the actual building, the actual lobby, the actual
  kids check-in desk. Not stock photos.
- Named contact: "If you have questions before Sunday, email Pastor
  Tim at tim@…"
- Kids ministry detail: age groups, security check-in, what a
  typical Sunday looks like for a 4-year-old.

**Language to avoid:**
- "Come as you are" without unpacking it (it's become noise)
- "We can't wait to meet you" as the only invitation
- Anything that implies they need to know the lingo before they show up

**Primary CTA shape:**
- "Plan your visit" → form captures name, email, kids' ages, the
  Sunday they're planning to attend. Confirmation email comes from
  a real person.

## Volunteer Lead

A current member or regular attender exploring how to serve. They
already trust the church; the question is *where* and *how much*.

**What they're afraid of:**
- Getting stuck on a team that doesn't fit
- Being shamed for stepping back later
- Committing to more than they have time for
- Walking into a team where everyone already knows each other

**What they want:**
- Clear time commitments
- A picture of what the role actually is, day-to-day
- Permission to try and step back if it isn't right

**Hooks that work:**
- Specific time asks: "Two Sundays a month, 45 minutes before service"
- Named team leader: "Jenna runs the kids check-in team. She'll
  reach out within 48 hours to walk you through it."
- A short list of what the role actually does — three bullets, not
  a paragraph
- "Try it once before you commit" framing where the role allows it
- Acknowledgment that life seasons change

**Language to avoid:**
- "We need volunteers!" (urgency framing reads as desperation)
- "Use your gifts" without specificity
- Heavy spiritual framing on a logistical page (save it for the
  ministry's about page)

**Primary CTA shape:**
- "Volunteer with [team name]" → form captures name, contact, any
  scheduling constraints. Lead reaches out personally.

## Multi-Site Pastor

A pastor researching how another church handles multi-site, planting,
or campus operations. Often visiting from another city; sometimes a
denominational rep. They're skimming the about page, the locations
page, the leadership page, and the giving / financial transparency
section.

**What they're afraid of:**
- Wasting time on a site that's all marketing
- Surface-level theology
- Inability to find the actual structure of the org

**What they want:**
- The bishop / network / denominational affiliation, stated clearly
- Names of campus pastors / lead pastors and their actual roles
- Service times across all locations on one page
- A statement of beliefs they can actually read

**Hooks that work:**
- Explicit structure: "We're three campuses under one elder team."
- Named leadership with photos and brief bios — not headshots only
- Network / denominational logos in the footer
- A "what we believe" page that reads like beliefs, not marketing
- Public financial summary or annual report linked from the giving
  page

**Language to avoid:**
- Vague "we're one church in multiple locations" without explaining
  what that means structurally
- Mission statements that could be any church's mission statement
- Hero copy that buries the church name

**Primary CTA shape:**
- This persona usually doesn't convert directly — they're researching.
  Make the about page, leadership page, and beliefs page easy to
  navigate. The "tour our facility" or "schedule a call with Pastor
  X" CTA is the rare conversion.

## Recently Relocated

Someone (single or coupled, often early-30s to mid-40s) who just
moved to the city and is church-shopping. Different from
Plan-a-Visit Family in that kids may not be the lens; community is.

**What they're afraid of:**
- Being a stranger forever
- Joining a church and never getting past the lobby
- Doctrinal mismatch they only discover six months in

**What they want:**
- A way into community fast — small groups, newcomer dinners,
  a path to belonging that doesn't depend on Sunday alone
- Belief clarity up front
- A sense of the city the church is in (do these people get my life?)

**Hooks that work:**
- "New to [city]?" framing on the homepage
- A clear path: visit Sunday → newcomer lunch → small group → serve
- Named pastor or community lead who personally reaches out
- City-specific copy that signals the church knows its neighborhood

**Language to avoid:**
- Generic "we'd love to meet you" without a specific next step
- "Come to a service" as the only entry point — community-seekers
  will bounce
- "Authentic community" / "doing life together" / other clichéd
  community language

**Primary CTA shape:**
- "Plan your visit" works, but a parallel "Join the next newcomer
  dinner" with a specific date converts this persona better.

## Faith-Curious / Returning

Someone who isn't currently a Christian, or who walked away from
faith years ago and is reconsidering. They're skeptical, possibly
wounded by previous church experience, and they bounce fast if the
copy reads as performative.

**What they're afraid of:**
- Being judged for where they are
- Pressure to convert, sign up, donate, or commit before they're ready
- Theological language they don't understand
- Hypocrisy

**What they want:**
- Permission to ask hard questions
- Honest answers, including "we don't know" where appropriate
- Space to attend without committing
- A way to engage that isn't Sunday morning if Sunday morning feels
  too high-stakes

**Hooks that work:**
- "If you're not sure what you believe, you're welcome here."
- Named pastor available for coffee / conversation, no agenda
- An Alpha-style or Christianity Explored offering, framed as
  "ask anything"
- Honest framing: "This isn't for everyone. Here's what we're about."
- Stories from real members about doubt, deconstruction, returning

**Language to avoid:**
- "Come find Jesus" as the entry point
- Salvation language on the first page
- Anything that implies the visitor is broken or lost — even when
  the theology underneath is true, the *framing* will lose them
- Performative "we're so excited to meet you!" warmth

**Primary CTA shape:**
- "Have coffee with a pastor" or "Ask us anything" — low-stakes,
  conversational, no commitment.

## Adding a persona

When a project's audience profile names an audience not covered
above, add a new section using the same shape:

- **Hero description** — who they are, when they hit the site
- **What they're afraid of** — the bounce triggers
- **What they want** — what success looks like for them
- **Hooks that work** — specific patterns
- **Language to avoid** — anti-patterns
- **Primary CTA shape** — what conversion actually looks like

Common additions to expect:
- Empty Nesters
- College Students
- Small-Group Leader
- Donor / Major Gift
- Spanish-Speaking Family (or other language-specific personas)
- Mental Health Seeker
- Recovery / Addiction
- Special-Needs Family

## What this doc is not

- **Not a marketing buyer-persona doc.** No demographic deep-dives,
  no income brackets, no media-consumption habits. Just the things
  that affect copy.
- **Not a substitute for the project's audience profile.** The
  intake captures *which* personas this church wants to reach. This
  doc tells the AI *how* to talk to them once selected.
- **Not exhaustive.** The personas above cover the most common church
  website audiences. Add new ones inline as projects surface them.
