# CMS Cross-Cutting Persuasive Patterns

Distilled from four shipped Church Media Squad church websites (1802 Mosaic, 1651 Mission Viejo Christian, 3585 Awaken Las Vegas, 2282 Real Life on the Palouse). These patterns are voice-agnostic — each church speaks differently, but the moves are the same.

## Four cross-cutting principles

1. **Every hero leads with the visitor's posture, not the church's claim about itself.** The h1 names a tension the visitor is already carrying. Past sites used "Broken Pieces, Made Whole" / "Stop searching, start belonging" / "Leave the polished version of yourself at home" — each one starting from where the visitor is. Going forward we put the tension/promise in the tagline + description, not the h1, but the principle is the same: visitor first, church second.

2. **The visitor is the protagonist; the church is the guide.** "You" appears in nearly every opening line. The church positions itself as the empathetic guide who has walked this road before, never as the hero. No paragraphs that read like a brag sheet.

3. **A specific felt-fear is named in the visitor's own words, then disarmed.** Kids = "Will my child be safe and want to come back?" Visit = "Will I be singled out / what do I wear?" Give = "Does my gift actually matter?" About = "Will I fit / what do they actually believe?" Naming the fear in the visitor's own words is the persuasive move; the answer is almost secondary.

4. **Every page closes with a low-friction next step framed as a story, not a transaction.** Nobody says "submit," "donate," or "sign up here." They say "join the story," "plan a visit," "find your place," "step in."

## Persuasive jobs by section type

### Hero (h1 + tagline + description + CTA)

Heading is always a clean page label or branded program name. Tagline qualifies (informational) or hooks (persuasive promise) or is omitted (utility pages). Description names the visitor's actual desire and promises the experience. CTA is direct and specific — no "Learn more."

### About / Our Story intro

Convert "should I trust these people?" into "I can see myself in this story." Frame the church not as an institution with credentials but as a movement with a reason for existing — usually tied to the visitor's own search for belonging, authenticity, or rootedness. Identity through history-with-grit, not polish.

### Kids page hero + intro

Convert parental anxiety into confidence by promising partnership rather than babysitting. Every kids hero leads with what the *parent* will feel, not what the kid will do. The kid's joy is the proof; the parent's peace of mind is the sale.

Past patterns: "You want your kids to love church, not just attend" (Mosaic) — "You want your children to have a faith of their own" (MVCC) — "Partnering with you to help your child love Jesus" (Real Life). Each addresses the parent (not the kid) → names the parent's want → promises partnership → safety proofs follow downstream.

### Plan a Visit hero + intro

Disarm the awkwardness of "I don't know what I'm walking into." Visit pages do not sell the church; they sell *the morning* — pre-loading the visitor with a mental movie of arriving, parking, dressing, sitting, leaving. Core move is permission: come as you are, no awkward moments, no expectation, no performance.

### Give page hero + intro

Reframe giving from "the church needs money" to "your generosity is the mechanism by which the mission becomes visible." Every give hero leads with *impact* or *invitation into a story*, never with need or obligation. Objection addressed: "does my gift actually matter?" Answered by linking dollars to specific lives changed.

### Care page hero + intro

Give permission to show up with the burden visible. This is not a place where you have to be okay. Name specific paths through specific hardships (recovery, grief, cancer, food, crisis) so the visitor finds their entry — and knows they're not the first one to need it here.

### Closing CTAs across pages

Invitational, not directive — extend an open hand rather than push a button. Pair primary conversion action with a secondary lower-commitment action. Frame the next step as the beginning of a story, not a transaction.

## Encoding shorthand

For any section that has emotional weight: {Acknowledge a felt tension or desire} → {Reframe the church/action as the posture-shift} → {Hand the visitor a single small step that feels like joining a story already in motion}.

The work happens in the tagline + description, NOT in the h1. The h1 stays a label.

## Brixies hero architecture (where these patterns land)

- **Heading slot (h1)** — always a clean page label or program name. Never a hook.
- **Tagline slot** — informational (factual qualifier) OR hook (persuasive promise) OR omit, per `tagline_strategy` from the brief.
- **Subheading slot** — optional supporting qualifier when tagline doesn't fully qualify.
- **Description slot** — the warm body where the persuasive prose lives. Addresses the section_job, names the visitor's desire, promises the experience.
- **Buttons** — invitational CTAs paired (primary + low-stakes secondary) when content supports.

Past CMS sites overloaded the h1 with hook lines. Going forward we don't.
